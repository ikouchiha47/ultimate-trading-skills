# 01 — Why an edge can exist

**The foundational question:** if markets price everything correctly, there is nothing to
trade. Our whole thesis rests on *why* exploitable dislocations survive.

## What it is
Three positions in tension:
- **EMH** (Fama): prices fully reflect available information; you cannot beat the market
  systematically. The null hypothesis we must beat.
- **Grossman–Stiglitz paradox**: *perfect* efficiency is impossible. If prices already
  reflected all information, no one would be paid to gather it — so no one would — so prices
  *couldn't* reflect it. Markets must be *inefficient enough* to compensate information
  gatherers. **This is the gap we trade.** Forced / uninformed selling (rotation, FII risk-off,
  redemptions) moves prices without information → fadeable dislocation.
- **Adaptive Markets** (Lo): reconciles the two — efficiency is not binary but evolves;
  edges appear, get arbitraged away, reappear as conditions change.

## Primary sources
- 📄 Fama (1970), *"Efficient Capital Markets: A Review of Theory and Empirical Work,"* J. Finance — the EMH statement.
- 📄 **Grossman & Stiglitz (1980), *"On the Impossibility of Informationally Efficient Markets,"* American Economic Review 70(3)** ← the key paper.
- 📕 Andrew Lo — *Adaptive Markets* (2017) — the reconciling modern view.
- 📕 O'Hara — *Market Microstructure Theory* — how order flow moves price.
- 📕 Bouchaud, Bonart, Donier & Gould — *Trades, Quotes and Prices* — modern price-impact / forced-selling.

## Where it fits
This is the *justification* for the entire strategy. The edge = telling **flow-driven**
dislocation (no information → reverts) from **information-driven** (real news → does not). If we
can't articulate why a given sell-off is uninformed, we don't have a trade.
